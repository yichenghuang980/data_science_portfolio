Estimate Effectiveness of Opioids Control Policies on Drug Overdose Mortality

November 5, 2020

Over the past two decades, the United States has seen a tremendous increase in the use and abuse of prescription opioids, leading not only to a huge rise in opioid addiction, but also a rise in prescription overdose deaths, and increasingly deaths from non-prescription opioids like heroin and fentanyl as people who became addicted to opioids due to prescriptions turn to illegal markets to sustain their addiction.

In order to deal with those situations, several states that had notoriety for having high opioids prescriptions and overdose mortality issued their own policies in different years. The goal of this project is to examine the effectiveness of those policies with pre-post analysis and difference-in-difference (DD) analysis. Three states with opioid control policies are Florida, Texas, and Washington. In order to construct control gorups and conduct DD analysis, three control states without such polices are chosen for each treated states based upon population, geographically proximity, and state government party.

Specifically, controll states for Florida:

Louisiana, Mississippi, South Carolina

For Texas:

Arkansas, New Mexico, Kansas

For Washington:

Colorado, Oregon, California

We mainly used two datasets:

  1. Opioid Prescriptions: a recently released dataset of all prescription opioid drug shipments in the United States from 2006 to 2012. This was released in mid-2019 by the Washington Post which obtained the data through a Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) request to the US Drug Enforcement Agency.

  2. Vital Statistics Mortality Data: the US Vital Statistics records which include data on every death in every county and every state in the United States.

Then we compare trends in both treated and control states before and after policy change to determine whether the policy truly impacted opioids shipments and drug overdose mortality. Here is an example:

It is noticeable that overdose mortality in Flordia and its control stats diverged from each other and the gap became even larger after the policy change, which indicates possible effectiveness of the opioid control policy in Floria.

We presume that due to the notoriety surrounding the incredibly large volumes of opioid prescribed in Florida, the policy in 2010 contains a series of mandatory regulations and physician requirements not seen in the other states. There is even a special task force established to deal with the Prescription Abuse. In contrast, the policies in Texas and Washington are one-time and there are no follow-up polices or institutions to oversee the implementation. Some of these guidelines are also merely recommendations that do not mandate physicians and medical institutions to comply with. Thus, the differences in the results the policies yielded across the states are understandable and foreseeable.

In general, the policies reviewed appear to be more effective in decreasing the volume of opioids shipment, and less effective in reducing overdose mortality. While there are likely many reasons behind this, some potential confounders include mental well being of drug addicts and the fact that restricting access to a drug does not address the underlying addiction that influences both demand for opioids and overdose mortality. Also these policies are only aimed at restricting the flow of prescription opioids. As a result, addicts might purchase and overdose on illegal opioids like heroin or fentanyl. This even has the potential to exacerbate the overdose problem as the unstandardized strength of illegal opioids is one of the causes for accidental overdoses.
